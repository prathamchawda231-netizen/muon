This application models the complex interaction of relativistic subatomic particles tracking through variable-density objects, reconstructing hidden 3D geometry and classifying internal material composition dynamically in the browser using JavaScript and Three.js.

## 🔬 Deep-Dive Physics & Algorithmic Architecture

Cosmic-ray muon tomography (muography) leverages high-energy atmospheric muons ($\approx 1\text{–}10\text{ GeV}$) generated by cosmic rays hitting the upper atmosphere. Because muons are highly penetrating leptons, they can pass through dense objects, but they scatter at wider angles when traversing materials with high atomic numbers ($Z$). By tracking the exact entry and exit vectors, we can map the interior of shielded, inaccessible volumes.

### 1. Multiple Coulomb Scattering & The Highland Formula
As a muon traverses matter, it undergoes numerous small-angle deflections driven by electromagnetic interactions with atomic nuclei. The simulation computes this cumulative deflection angle over a given trajectory step using a Gaussian approximation parameterized by the classical **Highland Formula**:

$$\theta_0 = \frac{13.6}{\beta p c} \sqrt{\frac{x}{X_0}} \left[1 + 0.038 \ln\left(\frac{x}{X_0}\right)\right]$$

Where:
*   $p$: Muon momentum ($\text{GeV/c}$), sampled dynamically from a cosmic ray energy spectrum.
*   $\beta c$: Particle velocity ($\beta \approx 1$ for relativistic cosmic muons).
*   $x$: The calculated continuous path length spent inside the intersecting geometry bounds.
*   $X_0$: The material-specific **Radiation Length**. The simulation engine references a localized lookup database:
    *   **Uranium ($Z=92$):** $X_0 \approx 0.32\text{ cm}$ (Extreme scattering density)
    *   **Tungsten ($Z=74$):** $X_0 \approx 0.35\text{ cm}$
    *   **Lead ($Z=82$):** $X_0 \approx 0.56\text{ cm}$
    *   **Iron ($Z=26$):** $X_0 \approx 1.76\text{ cm}$
    *   **Water / Organics:** $X_0 \approx 36.1\text{ cm}$ (Minimal scattering deflection)

### 2. Point of Closest Approach (PoCA) Reconstruction
To reconstruct where the scattering event occurred without prior knowledge of the hidden object, the engine runs a **Point of Closest Approach (PoCA)** algorithm for every muon event:

*   **Vector Construction:** The top 4 tracker planes define an incoming 3D ray $\vec{R}_{in}(t) = \vec{P}_{in} + t\vec{D}_{in}$. The bottom 4 tracker planes define an outgoing skewed ray $\vec{R}_{out}(s) = \vec{P}_{out} + s\vec{D}_{out}$.
*   **Vertex Minimization:** Because the two rays rarely intersect perfectly in 3D space due to scattering and detector noise, the algorithm minimizes the distance vector $\vec{V} = \vec{R}_{in}(t) - \vec{R}_{out}(s)$ by setting the derivative of the squared distance to zero:
    $$\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\vert{}\vec{V}\vert{}^2 = 0, \quad \frac{\partial}{\partial s}\vert{}\vec{V}\vert{}^2 = 0$$
*   **Voxel Grid Mapping:** The calculated midpoint vertex $(x, y, z)$ of the shortest line segment connecting the two rays is mapped into a discrete $40 \times 40 \times 40$ 3D spatial voxel buffer. Voxels that repeatedly accumulate high-angle PoCA vertices increase in density value, visually exposing the hidden structure.

### 3. Maximum-Likelihood Softmax Material Classification
Rather than just identifying shapes, the application infers elemental composition by running a statistical classifier on the accumulated scattering distributions within active voxels:
*   The system models scattering angles using a **Rayleigh Distribution** representing the expected angular deviation for a given material class.
*   As events stream in, the system maintains a running log-likelihood profile for each possible target material.
*   These raw likelihood values are passed through a stabilized **Softmax Function** to output normalized, real-time probability confidences ($0\text{--}100\%$) displaying exactly what material is being scanned.
